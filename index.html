Top 5 Smartphone Photography Tips and Tricks for 2026

Written on March 12, 2026 | By Editorial Team

Smartphones now capture over 92% of all photographs taken【49†L98-L102】. In fact, the average person uses their phone to take a photo about six times each day【49†L102-L105】. With mobile cameras becoming our default photography tool, mastering a few simple techniques can turn ordinary snapshots into stunning images. The tips below will help you make the most of your phone’s lenses and modes to take sharper, more creative photos.

"If your pictures aren’t good enough, you’re not close enough." – Robert Capa【46†L151-L154】

Don’t Hesitate to Zoom for Macro Shots

Most phones automatically switch to a macro mode when you get very close to a subject, but you can take it further by using digital zoom. Adding a bit of zoom “enhances the framing of the shot” without sacrificing closeness【34†L840-L847】【50†L853-L856】. In practice, this means you can tightly fill the frame with your subject and avoid having to literally touch the lens to the object. As one expert notes, a slight digital zoom “still get[s] very close” to the subject without the lens casting shadows or glare on it【50†L853-L856】. In other words, zooming in just a little can give you a cleaner, better-composed macro photo.

Use Your Telephoto Lens for Parallax Effects

For scenes where you want more background blur or a dramatic effect, switch to your phone’s telephoto (zoom) lens. By default we tend to use the main wide camera, but the telephoto lens “can do a number of things, like giving sprawling cityscapes a parallax effect or filling more of the frame with a subject”【50†L897-L905】. In practical terms, zooming in around 5× to 10× will exaggerate the foreground-background separation. This makes portraits especially striking (the background blurs more) and also “fills in more of the scene” for landscapes【50†L897-L905】. In short, don’t be afraid to zoom in far when your phone offers it – you’ll often get a more professional look with extra depth and focus.

Maximize Night Mode for Long-Exposure Detail

When shooting in low light or at night, take advantage of your camera’s long-exposure modes. Most phones automatically enter a “night mode” that uses a slower shutter speed. Some even offer an extended mode: for example, the iPhone 17 Pro has a “night mode max” that holds the shutter open up to 30 seconds【50†L940-L948】. By mounting your phone on a tripod (or holding it very steady) during these ultra-long exposures, you can “draw out way more detail” in dark scenes【50†L978-L981】. In practice, this means that street lights, stars, or dim interiors will look much clearer and more vivid. The key is to keep the camera still and let the phone gather light – the results can be dramatically better than a quick snapshot.

Experiment with Vertical Panoramas

We usually pan side-to-side for panoramas, but try shooting a panorama by moving the phone up and down instead. This vertical panorama technique turns tall scenes (like skyscrapers or trees) into creative compositions【50†L997-L1004】. The result can look almost surreal – imagine capturing a building so tall that the image wraps around it in an “Inception-esque” distortion【50†L1000-L1005】. For best results, keep the framing tight so distant edges don’t warp too much. With practice, vertical panoramas let you showcase height and depth in a way standard photos can’t.

Flip Your Phone for a New Perspective

Finally, don’t overlook composition tricks. One technique is to flip your phone upside down (so the lens is near the bottom) and shoot from a lower angle. This simple change can make ordinary subjects look dramatic and symmetrical【43†L1047-L1054】. For example, holding the phone low and flipped can turn a simple railing or walkway into an abstract pattern. As a Tom’s Guide editor demonstrated, flipping the camera “gives it a more abstract, symmetrical feel” and a more dramatic look overall【43†L1047-L1054】. In short, shooting with the lens closer to the ground – even by inverting the phone – can yield surprisingly artistic results.

By applying these tips and tricks, you’ll be well on your way to taking more impressive photos with your phone. Whether you’re a casual shooter or a budding mobile photographer, these techniques leverage the full power of modern phone cameras to improve composition, clarity, and creativity.
